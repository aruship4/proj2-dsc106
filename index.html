Proposition: Lower fertility rates reflect better economic conditions.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Design decision 1: Strong claim-driven title and subtitle Score: -1.0 I used the title Lower Fertility Signals Stronger Economic Stability and a subtitle that emphasizes a strong positive relationship between adolescent fertility and average vulnerable employment. This makes the graph more persuasive because it frames the relationship as evidence of economic strength before the reader studies the distribution of points. This worked well because it sounds confident and evidence-based without appearing obviously extreme. I considered a more cautious title using “is associated with,” but I chose stronger language because it better supports the proposition.
  • Design decision 2: Recent-year filtering Score: -0.5 I limited the graph to the latest available country-level values since 2015, which makes the relationship appear more current and more stable across countries. This helps persuasion by reducing temporal noise and making the chart feel more relevant to present-day economic conditions.This worked well because it produced a cleaner comparison and avoided mixing old labor conditions with newer ones. I considered including all available years, but that would have made the chart noisier and less focused.
  • Design decision 3: Bold black summary line across fertility deciles Score: -0.5 I added a thick black line connecting the median vulnerable employment values across 10 fertility groups. This makes the pattern look smoother, more directional, and more authoritative than the raw country cloud alone. This worked well because viewers naturally follow the line first and read it as the main story of the chart. I considered using only the raw points or a regression line, but the decile summary felt more distinctive and persuasive while still looking analytical.
  • Design decision 4: Light blue points with low emphasis Score: 1.0 I colored the country dots a soft light blue and kept them visually faint compared with the black summary line. This preserves the full dataset while subtly guiding attention toward the summarized trend instead of toward outliers or noisy variation. This worked well because the graph still looks clean and credible, but the supporting evidence stays in the background. I considered darker or multicolored points, but those pulled attention away from the message I wanted the viewer to notice.
  • Design decision 5: Selective country labeling and annotation Score: -0.5 I labeled a few recognizable countries at the low- and high-fertility ends, such as Japan, Switzerland, Niger, Chad and added annotations that explicitly describe the pattern. This makes the relationship easier to remember and pushes the viewer toward the intended takeaway that lower fertility aligns with lower labor vulnerability. This worked well because named examples and short explanatory notes make the graph feel more concrete and easier to interpret. I considered leaving the graph unlabeled or labeling many more countries, but that either weakened the rhetoric or made the figure too cluttered.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Truncated Y-axes (-1.5): Both axes start well above zero (fertility starts at 4, unemployment starts at 7), exaggerating the steepness of both trends. A reader unfamiliar with dual-axis conditions will perceive the divergence as far more dramatic than it actually is. Starting both axes at zero was considered but rejected because it flattened the visual story entirely, making the opposing trends nearly imperceptible.
  • Country Selection (Greece, Italy, Spain, Portugal) (-1): Filtering to Southern Europe, and aggregating the measures into one line for each measurement, was framed as a geographic choice, but it is fundamentally a data filtration choice. These 4 countries experienced the sharpest post-2008 unemployment spikes in Europe while also having declining fertility, making them ideal for the opposition argument. Germany and the Netherlands, which are similarly low-fertility countries but maintained low unemployment, were excluded from this visualization.
  • Implied Causation via Dual-Axis Layout (-0.5): Placing two opposing trends on the same chart with mirrored axes suggests a causal or correlational relationship without explicitly claiming one. The visualization never states that falling fertility causes rising unemployment, but the visual layout can lead readers to infer it, leading to a score of -0.5, slight deception.
  • Recession Band Annotation (+0.5): Labeling the shaded region “Financial Crisis" is the most earnest decision in the chart, disclosing the confounding variable that explains both trends. However, it can’t earn a full 2 because it’s placed subtly and framed as context, leading to readers absorbing it as a helper to the argument. It adds credibility without undermining persuasion.

Final Reflection

To fully understand the claims we were making, we implemented the usage of a dataset that was based around human development and quality of life. Our baseline became a multitude of variables that weren't strictly metric, requiring us to make interpretive choices about which indicators best captured economic conditions. While there was a lot of data that was missing, fertility rates and employment vulnerability provided enough to draw meaningful conclusions about economic development. These two variables allude to broader structural conditions, reflecting access to stable work, gender equity in labor markets, and long-term economic opportunity.

When looking at the economic prosperity of a region, it is apparent that many factors contribute to this. The factor we decided to focus on was fertility rates, as it is shaped by many other factors like job security, income stability, and workforce participation. Our proposition was that lower fertility rates reflect stronger economic conditions. Our proposition visualization reinforces this by showing a strong positive correlation of r = 0.74 between adolescent fertility and average vulnerable employment, with low-fertility countries like Japan and Switzerland clustering near the bottom of the labor vulnerability scale. On the other hand, the opposing claim, that lower fertility is associated with adverse economic conditions, is backed up by trends seen in Southern Europe. Between 2005 and 2019, falling fertility coincided with unemployment surging past 21% after the 2008 financial crisis.

It's important to provide sufficient context and reasoning for any filtering or transformation decisions in order to conduct ethical data analysis and visualization. Working through this project made us realize how much design choices mediate the story data tells — truncating axes, filtering to specific countries, adding summary lines, and framing titles all shifted what a reader would conclude without changing any underlying data. A visualization is not ethical simply because it uses real data, but because it honestly represents what has been filtered or transformed. Persuasion through framing and emphasis is unavoidable; concealing contrary evidence or implying causation where only correlation exists is where acceptable persuasion becomes misleading.
